1 Production & Global products

1.1 Outsourcing as a key component of globalization

  • China, workshop of the world since 1990s
  • Low cost labour
  • Results in technology transfers from western countries to China
  • Makes TNCs more competitive while enabling the development of the workshop countries
  • Product is globalized when its components are produced around the world, and by different firms
    • Modular system

1.2 Global production networks

  • GPN, chain of connected suppliers where parts move around
    • Contains the actors of globalization
    • Big TNCs have giant GPNs, tens of thousands of suppliers
    • Different from the division of labour within the

1.2.1 Example: Mini

BMW: 2500 different suppliers

  • Some parts are from the EU, less tariffs
  • Engine is from Brazil, costs less to produce
  • Parts made by contractors who themselves have a global network

1.2.2 Example: iPhone

Outsourcing creates a long chain, example with Apple's screen:

  1. Apple, USA
  2. Foxconn, Taiwan → factory in China
  3. Wintek, Taiwan → outsources to China's Lianjian
  4. Raw materials come from around the globe

1.2.3 Example: Fender musical instruments

  • Two types of products:
    • Low-cost outsourced production in Mexico: helped by NAFTA
    • High-cost, better quality, local production by skilled workers in the US
      • "post-fordism", "Made in USA"
  • Outsourcing countries have become a market in themselves, thanks to cultural globalization and the country's economic development

1.2.4 Example: Facebook

  • 1.6B users throughout the globe, userbase growth thanks to developing countries
  • Mega-corporation, many acquisitions: owns a significant part of the communication & media (Facebook, Whatsapp, Instagram, etc.)
  • Aided by growth in technology, reduction in smartphone prices, etc.
  • Established a big physical network
    • 70 regional offices, present on every continent
    • Invests by creation of:
      • Regional offices for sales and advertising
      • Regional data storage facilities (Sweden has the biggest server), ex. of influence of physical geography

2 Trade Blocks & Free Trade zones

2.1 Trade blocks

  • MGO, multi-governmental organizations
  • removal or decrease of internal tarrifs
  • Benefits:
    • Markets grow and firms get new customers
    • Specific firms that are geographically locked (like wine-makers) are not hampered by that anymore within the block
    • More demand → more production → lower prices
    • TNCs within the bloc can merge for better effectiveness (Airbus, Airtel Africa)
    • Trade barriers are stronger, making the EU more powerful in blocking selected imports (compared to its individual members)
    • Blocks can be formed of states at different development stages, which benefits TNCs as they can do everything (except for sales) within the block (USA + Mexico for ex.)

Major trade blocks:

  • America
    • NAFTA
    • Pacific Alliance
    • Mercosur
    • Caricom
  • Europe
    • EU
  • Africa
    • COMESA, SADC, UEMOA
  • Asia
    • ASEAN

Companies like Unilever, Ikea have expanded significantly thanks to the EU trade block

  • Both in terms of production and sales within the block

However, member countries still hold restrictions on some sectors

2.2 Free Trade Zones (Zones Economiques Spéciales)

Special Economic Zones (SEZ)_: Industrial area, usually coastal, with low tax and no tariffs, created to incite TNCs to come and invest

  • India, started in 1965, now >200
  • China: crucial to its growth in the Deng Xiaoping era, they enabled the country to progress economically but also technologically through the "join ventures"
    • 50% of GDP from these places in the 1990s
  • Indonesia creates a zone for raw materials
    • Attracts many clothing TNCs

3 Shrinking World

3.1 Technologies

  • Exponential growth of ICT (Information + Communication tech) is an outcome and a driver of globalization
    • But there is also war and security: WW1+2 led the extremely rapid development of the aeronautic and space industry, but also computers, radar, nuclear, etc.
    • Social need can drive technology, but more importantly it is technological progress that shapes our societies
  • Leads to time-space compression
    • Physically, through plane travel & low cost airlines that make travelling much more accessible
      • Economic shrinking, money can be invested easily, containers standardization enables mass shipping, etc.
    • Abstractly, through the speed at which data travels - almost instant
      • Internet, etc.
    • Innovation is driven by governments (ARPANET), hobbyists (modem), TNCs and their constant need to improve

5 Cultural Change

5.1 Cultural traits

  • Language, dialects
  • Food, traditional dishes
  • Clothing
  • Religion
  • Traditions

5.2 Global scale

  • Globalization has a heavy influence on culture: Westernization, spread of the English language
    • Also more local influences: China & Tibet, Uyghur, etc.
    • soft power
    • Language: 40% of the world's pop speaks one of these 8 major languages
      • Small languages are also rapidly declining, for ex. in Russia or in South America
  • Factors:
    • TNCs are the main vector through which culture spreads
    • Global media: Disney films, Japanese Pokemon, etc.
    • Migration & Tourism: diffusion of culture

6 Resistance to globalization

6.1 Sustainability and Justice

  • Impact that globalization has on the environment
  • Social inequality that globalization emphasizes and doesn't solve
  • TNCs are often targeted, as they are immensely rich and often avoid taxes (legally) through smart placement in key countries

6.2 Nationalism and sovereignty

  • People feel like their jobs are being "stolen", that they are the losers of globalization
  • Nationalism is on the rise, in the EU for example
    • Sovereignty but also identity and financial concerns

6.3 Problems with TNCs

  • Immense wealth, increase of the wealth gap, gives them a lot of power, especially of development
  • Social harm: people working for very low wages, etc.
  • Environmental degredation
  • Tax avoidance
  • Cultural imperialism

6.3.1 Examples

  • 1999, WTO conferences, 40k people protest against how developed countries are controlling globalization
  • 2003: Farmer suicides to denounce trade laws
  • Canadian oil exploitation led to death of trouts due to pollution, and spread alcohol and drugs to local populations
    • Fracking practices too
  • Protests against McDonald in India
    • Local cultures are extinguished by the uniformity of McDonald's restaurants
    • Local cooking traditions are lost, languages too
    • Despite opposition, such as the creation of some restaurants in sacred places, it has had a lot of success (>300 million customers in 2015)

6.4 Local sourcing

  • Attempt to boycott imports by buying from local suppliers
    • Reduced the food miles, give a more secure source of food, less polution (especially through the removal of air travel)
  • Problems:
    • They might still travel a lot, through distribution centers etc.
    • Local transport is less efficient than large ships or trucks
    • Local food production systems can be more energy intensive
    • Buying global food helps other countries develop, in Africa for ex.

6.5 Anti-immigration

  • Goes with nationalism, rise in EU countries
  • Impacts:
    • Economic impacts, migrants are key to the US's economy yet unemployment makes people angry
    • National security, banning every Muslim will solve terrorism !
    • Demographic impact, help counter the aging population
    • Cultural change: considerable influence on local customs

6.6 Protectionism

  • Slowdown of globalization, 2008 crisis → doesn't rise again like expected
  • Protectionism goes against the free flow of people and goods, adds higher tariffs
  • Governments intervene in trade deals:
    • Trans-Pacific Partnership is not ratified yet, and might never be as political support fades
    • Western countries are calling for limits on Chinese investment → raising tariffs on imports
    • Countries are blocking acquisitions of national companies or places (hotels, etc.) to limit "infiltration"
  • Also a result of concerns about ecology (fear of importing diseases) and politics (Cuba & US)

(Interesting, great firewall can be seen as a trade barrier for e-shopping and therefore can be denounced as unfair competition → which would break the WTO's rules)
