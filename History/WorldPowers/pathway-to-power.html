2 Background

In 1890, the US's navy was smaller than Sweden or Turkey

  • Foreign policy shaped the US's power

Monroe Doctrine (1823):

  • Separate the US and European spheres of influence (non-colonization)
    • New World vs Old World
  • They didn't want to interfere anywhere → isolationism

Our business with them (Europe), and theirs with them is commerce, not politics and much less war

3 1918 → 1945 - From isolationism to interventionism

3.1 WW1, the attempt to impose a peace settlement

Tried to stay neutral

  • Made bank loans to both sides ($2.3B allies, $23M axis)
  • Traded with both sides
  • At the end, a lot of debt from other countries come in

However… 2 reasons:

  • Germany's submarine warfare was threatening US ships
  • Zimmerman (Germany's foreign secretary) telegram sent to Mexico to help

1917: Declared war on Germany

Sent 2M men, lost 50k (few compared to for ex British lose 20k first day of Somme)

Wilson's 14 points:

  • Tried to setup a peace treaty, self-determination
  • Setup the League of Nations
    • But Republicans are very unhappy and win majority in Congress → no one wants to go in League of Nations

Four big:

  • Wilson, Clemenceau, Orlando, Lloyd George

3.2 1920-1941: Power without obligation / The US preserves its interest

3.2.1 Return to isolationism: Never Again !

  • Has become interventionist, but American people want to go back to isolationism
  • Wanted to be internationally uninvolved, but trade forced them into political matters
    • Politics and money couldn't be separated
  • Consumerism, Fordism, development → US is doing fine
  • Majority of WASP
    • Hostile to Catholics, restrict immigration, turn to themselves
  • Only economic intervention in Europe

United States … .. . (France → US, pay back loans) .. .. (Germany → US, pay back loans) .. . .. . .. .. France ………………. Germany

(Germany → France, pay back reparations)

  • Kellogg-Brian Pact: War was declared illegal (???)
  • Simson Doctrine: Doesn't recognize Japan's invasion of Manchuria

3.2.2 Power without obligation

Washington Armament Conference: reduce military strength, so reduce the risk of going to war

  • 1921-1922: building diplomatic power in Europe
  • Wanted to prevent Japanese invasion in China

1933: Good neighbor policy, promises no military intervention

  • Recognition of the USSR

Rise of fascism in Europe:

  • Congress tries to pass laws to avoid getting involved in any potential conflict that would happen

Neutrality acts:

  • 1935: prohibit the sales of arms and munitions to countries
  • 1936: prohibit loans to countries at war
  • 1937: extends to civil war (Spanish Civil war)

However, in FDR's annual speech in January 1941:

  • Promises freedom from fear, to free speech, from necessity, everywhere in the world

3.2.3 The end of isolationism & intervention / from biased neutrality to war

November 1939, weapons can be sold again to UK & France in order to ensuire their safety 1940: France's defeat leads to restoring conscription March 1941: Lend-Lease, weapons can be lent to US's allies Aug 1941: Atlantic charter, set the bases for the UN → FDR + Churchill

December 1941: USA at war on 2 fronts

Victory Program-me: mobilize US economy and industry fully

4 The USA during the Cold War

4.1 The USA disseminates its power in a bi-polar world

Plan for International Cooperation War Time Conferences (Diplomatic / Economic Power)

  • Bretton woods, cf. International Governance
  • US leads the way (vs Keynes)

Terrain Conference:

  • Agreed on the UN and weak post-war Germany (what the USSR wanted)

Yalta:

  • Agreements made, USSR goes to war against Japan, etc

Potsdam:

  • Allied control of defeated Germany and reparations
  • Reparations issue
  • Agreed on Poland's borders
  • Russia joined war on far East

US is always in the middle in the pictures

4.2 The start of the Cold War - US Unilateral Intervention

Marshall Plan: economic power Berlin Blockade: hard, military power National Security Act:: Establishes NSC, Department of Defense, CIA NSC-68: Allowed expansion of military spending ($13B → $45B)

  • US is fully involved in Cold War

1947: Rio Pact 1949: NATO 1954: SEATO, Southeast Asia Treaty Organization 1955: CENTO, (Baghdad Pact)

1952: US has H-Bombs, USSR does too

1957 Sputnik launched

  • Creation of NASA

1960: France tests A-Bomb

4.2.1 Unilateral intervention in the 50s

Asia: Korean war (1950-1953) (UN forces but mostly US) Middle-East: Suez

1957: Eisenhower doctrine, US will defend the Middle East against Soviet attack

  • Lebanon, US sent 14k troops in Lebanon

4.2.2 Difficulties in the 60s and 70s

Asia: Vietnam War Latin America: Cuba Middle East: Yom Kippur (Oil ?)

Massive Retaliation, Eisenhower ("more bang for the buck", hit hard with the money)

  • Brinkmanship
  • Domino theory

Kennedy: "flexible response", wants to prevent war

  • Diplomatic solution to Cuban missile crisis

Vietnam:

  • Failure of the military
  • Govt hid what was going on in the beginning
  • Deception in government

4.2.3 70s Nixon

  • End of Vietnam War (Catastrophic Failure)
  • Opens doors with China (ping-pong diplomacy, communication, trades)

é - Kissinger: "political realism", foreign policy should be based only on power, not ideals or moral principles

  • Visits China in 1972
  • Carter recognizes China in 1979
  • New balance of power: "vietnamization"
    • However continued confrontation with communist states

Decline in power in the 70s

Periods of detente

SALT: Strategic Arms Limitation Talks

  • USA and USSR agreed to not make new ABM (anti-ballistic) missiles

SALT II not ratified because of invasion of Afghanistan

Middle East: Shuttle Diplomacy

  • Kissinger travelling back and forth between Israel and Egypt

1975: Final Vietnam act signed

Carter Presidency:

  • Detente + Human rights
  • Concerned with USSR's aid to Third World Communists
  • However Carter's foreign policy was a mess:
    • Iranian revolution, Soviet invasion of Afghanistan, conservative revolution
      • → not really much detente left

4.2.4 Reagan

USSR is the "Evil Empire"

SDI: Strategic Defense Initiative, Star Wars

  • Military spending goes up by $100B during Reagan's First Term
  • Reaffirm the US power → end of detente
  • Against SALT and very expensive and not feasible

1986: Iran-Contra Scandal US had sold arms to Shah in Iran

  • To win the realease of US citizens held in Lebanon by pro-Iranian groups (even if they said)
  • Money won from these sales went to the contra in Nicaragua (funding anti communist guerrillas)

Gorbachev came to power (1985)

  • Soviet Union couldn't keep up with the weapons
  • Introduced Glasnost and Perestroika
