4.1 The development of the press

The Golden Age (1880 - 1914)

Press became the 1st mass media

  • Republican success
  • New liberal regime led to proliferating of newspapers (79 in Paris and 257 in rest of France)
  • Progress in printing techniques + distribution
    • Prices go down → copies go up
  • Development of education and literacy → more people reading

Massive explosion of numbers of titles (over 300 in 1914) and size of readership (9 million copies printed in 1914)

Different kinds of press:

  • Neutral information: Le Petit Journal, le Petit Parisien
  • Opinionated press:
    • Far left: Humanité
    • Left: L'Aurore
    • Right: Le Figaro
    • Far right: Action Française, La Libre Parole

Freedom of opinion remains fragile (!= freedom of press)

  • Press was influenced
  • Financed by businesses, reflected manager's opinions
  • Frequent corruption
  • 1892: Panama Scandal
    • Investors that bought shares in the panama canal company lost it, because they were influenced by the media which had been bribed by the company

4.2 Popular press vs Quality Press

  • Lucrative business: meet the public demand rather than influence the political opinion
  • Main: Petit Parisien, Petit Journal → contain more random, misc stuff than actual important content

The political journals have far less:

  • Action française 300k
  • Aurore (Zola, J'Accuse)

4.3 Dreyfus Affair

Press revealed the affair in 1894: at the time the press was very popular

  • Claims of espionnage dealt with in the military court, in secret
  • Affair broke out when the real culprit, Esterhazy, was discovered: but he wasn't condemned
  • Zola writes "J'accuse" to lead to a real trial
  • Media and population divided → press led th investigations
  • Lead press was Libre Parole, Action Française → went against Jews, anti-semitic
  • Dreyfusard were the intellectuals, defend human rights & fix an injustice

La libre parole talked about it in 1894, yet it was supposed to be secret:

  • The press is active and gets involved

1898: Anti Dreyfussard still dominant and remained, and public opinion mostly against dreyfus

  • only 32% of newspapers in favor of a revision of the trial (boosted by J'Accuse)
  • Petit Journal is supposed to be neutral, but presents very anti-dreyfusard views ("Vive l'armée")

Newspapers created:

  • le Sifflet (satirical) (1898), Dreyfussard, in response to "Psst…!" which was anti-dreyfussard
  • Many newspapers created just during the affairs

1906: Dreyfus found innocent

4.4 Newspapers during WW1

Press at War: Back to Censorship

State controlled newspapers:

  • To avoid hurting troop morale
  • To avoid publishing military secrets
  • Vector of propaganda
  • Show patriotism
    • Brainwashing

4.4.1 Anastasia or the Press at War

  • Uion Sacrée & Anastasie: government censorship introduced to keep popular morale high
  • Press presents highly misleading & optimistic view of the front leading to alienation
  • Reaction: Creation of Canard Enchaîné to point out satirically political scandals and censorship c

5 Media Post WW1

5.1 The Rise of Radio and Film

Competition: Radio and Cinema

  • 1912: Radio developped
  • 1932: 10% of the French have access to a radio
  • 1939: 56% of the French have access to a radio
  • Very much controlled by the government

Modernization of the Press:

  • Despite decline in numbers of daily titles, over 11m prints in 1939
  • Press changes nature: magazines appear, weekly etc
    • Provincial press gains importance

Advent of the radio

  • First regular broadcast: radio "Tour Eiffel"
  • First private radio: Radio Paris (1922)
  • Used by politics for campaigns in 1936

5.2 1934 Crisis

5.2.1 Background instability

  • Instability since WW1, inflation & high taxation
  • Impact of Great Depression + Rise of Fascist States
    • Unemployment soared

Political instability:

  • Rise of the extreme right (Action Française, Faisceau)
  • Afraid of Bolshevism
  • Growth of xenophobia

Political affairs which discredit the Republic

  • Panama Affair
  • Boulanger Crisis

5.2.2 Role of the press

Press was violent

Example: Stavisky affair

  • Business man that stole money with deputy and newspaper directors
  • Bribed his way out of arrestations
  • 1934: found dead - but he knew many things that would've been helpful
    • Claims suicide, press divided
  • Effect: Chaumtemps, prime minister quits - Daladier replaces him and fires préfet de police
  • ⇒ political instability

→ Leads to parliamentary crisis, "ras-le-bol" 6th of February 1934: Right-wing demonstrations

  • "A bas les ministres et les députés vendus !" - Action Française
  • It is the press who calls people to protest on the front page
    • The day when Daladier is inaugurated
    • Newspaper's director is Maurras, royalist
    • Insulting, strong vocabulary: violent press movement
    • Action Française prints triple/quadruples

Left vs Right

  • The Left pointed out the link between Stavisky and Chiappe (Salengro Affair)
  • Right turned Stavisky in a symbol of corruption

Gathering at place de la Concorde in front of Assemblée Nationale

  • Degenerated: protesters tried to storm into the Palais Bourbon and clash with police
  • 14 dead, hundreds of casualties
  • Le Populaire: "Le coup de force fasciste a échoué"
  • Consequence: government shook, creation of the "Front Populaire"

Salengro Affair

  • Gringoire, far right paper that attacked politicians on personal levels
  • 1936, started a defamation campaign against minister Roger Salengro who committed suicide as a consequence of this harassment
  • Le Populaire: "Ils l'ont tué ! […] Le Fascisme assassin"

Radio was the #1 form of media:

  • Back to censorship
  • Pétain speaks through radios, and also De Gaulle
  • Radio because a political tool:
    • Radio Paris / Radio Londres, les français parlent aux français

6 Media 1944-1974

6.1 The Press at the Liberation

  • Freedom restored after WW2
  • New Golden Age: creation of FranceSoir, Le Monde, AFP (Agence France Presse - give all media equal and impartial information)
    • Paper that supported Vichy were banned
    • Papers were subsidised - so that there's no lobbying
    • Later l'Express (1953) on the model of weekly US magazines
    • Press starts to decline (daily papers) → in favor of radio

6.2 Audiovisual under State Controlled

  • Press was free
  • However the State imposed tight control over Radio & TV
    • RTF (Radiodiffusion télévision française) / ORTF
    • Public media didn't broadcast advertising, however in exchange was under control
  • Development of peripheral stations (especially after 68)
    • Europe 1, RTL
